Where did human rights come from?

They arose because a person, in addition to the physical, also has a moral and spiritual essence. Human rights are needed in order to protect and preserve the human essence of everyone, in order to provide each person with a decent life – the life that a person deserves.

The Committee against Torture

The Committee against Torture is a Russian non-profit organization working in the field of human rights protection. Currently, the Committee has the official status of an interregional public organization.

The Committee was founded in 2000 by a number of well-known Nizhny Novgorod human rights defenders as a human rights organization whose task was to exercise public control over the situation related to the problem of the spread of the practice of torture and ill-treatment in Russia and to provide professional legal and medical assistance to victims of torture. The starting point of the activities of the Committee against Torture can be considered the work of the Information and Analytical Center of the Nizhny Novgorod Society for Human Rights, and, in particular, its "Report on the use of torture in the Nizhny Novgorod region", presented in December 1997 by the legal. The report did not achieve the main goal set by the authors — to draw the attention of the prosecutor's office to systematic lawlessness and encourage them to take active actions to prevent and solve this type of crime. This was the reason for the creation in 2000 of an independent organization under the leadership of I. A. Kalyapin — public interest and the principle of focusing on domestic rather than international protection mechanisms.As of March 2019 (since the foundation of the Committee in 2000), the organization has verified more than two thousand received allegations of human rights violations, has achieved the award of more than seventy million rubles in compensation to victims of torture and the conviction of more than one hundred and forty law enforcement officials, has established one hundred and eighty-nine facts of torture, overturned eight hundred and eighty-five illegal decisions and won thirty-six complaints in the European Court of Human Rights

The principle of protecting the public interest is that the organization conducting the investigation does not primarily represent the interests of a particular person - a victim of torture or other gross violation of human rights — but the public interest. The Committee does not undertake any obligation to help the victim if she later wishes to enter into bargaining with the alleged perpetrators or their representatives. Moreover, if in the future the victim changes his testimony in favor of the alleged perpetrators, and by this time the Committee will have convincing evidence of torture, the Committee reserves the right to seek criminal prosecution of both the perpetrators and the victim herself for giving deliberately false testimony and concealing a crime The principle of preferential orientation to domestic protection mechanisms. The main efforts of the public investigation are aimed at making the domestic mechanisms for the protection of human rights work effectively and in accordance with the law. In this scheme, recourse to international mechanisms is considered as a "last resort", which should be used only if all possible ways to achieve justice within the country within a reasonable time are exhausted.
